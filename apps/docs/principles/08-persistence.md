# 8. Persistence — The Sustained Effort Necessary to Induce Faith

> *"There is one quality which one must possess to win, and that is definiteness of purpose — the knowledge of what one wants, and a burning desire to possess it."* — Napoleon Hill

## What It Is

Persistence is to the character of man what carbon is to steel. It is the direct product of will-power combined with desire. Without persistence, you cannot successfully master any of the other principles.

The majority of people who fail do so because they quit at the first sign of opposition. The story of R.U. Darby — who stopped drilling **three feet from a vein of gold** — is the most cited lesson on persistence in success literature.

## Key Insight

Most people who fail do so because they quit when temporary defeat strikes. "Three feet from gold" is the greatest lesson in persistence in all literature.

## The Four Steps

::: tip Action Steps
1. **Build a definite purpose** backed by burning desire — this is the foundation of persistence.
2. **Develop a definite plan** expressed in continuous action — even imperfect plans, consistently executed, beat perfect plans that are not.
3. **Close your mind** to all negative and discouraging influences, including well-meaning but fearful friends and family.
4. **Form a friendly alliance** with at least one person who will encourage you without reservation.
:::

## The Darby Lesson

R.U. Darby and his uncle were mining for gold in Colorado. They struck a vein, worked it, and then — as suddenly as it appeared — the vein seemed to disappear. They drilled for weeks. Nothing. Defeated, they sold their equipment for a few hundred dollars.

The junk dealer who bought the equipment hired a mining engineer. The engineer calculated that the vein was exactly **three feet from where Darby had stopped**.

Darby later became one of the most successful life insurance salesmen of his era. He turned the three-feet-from-gold lesson into a philosophy: **never, never quit.**

## Daily Affirmation

*"I persist through every obstacle. Defeat is a signal to learn and adjust, never to abandon."*
